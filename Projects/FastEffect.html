AR Presenter: AR Research/Design Internship

Project Outcome

AR Presenter

This project produced "AR Presenter": a Hololens-based augmented reality application designed to facilitate the trade/tech show experience. At the highest level, the product mimics the paradigm of Powerpoint, in that it allows presenters to build a "presentation" out of individual components, which can then be presented to viewers in a controlled manner. However, in this product, the "slides" are AR objects with triggerable interactions, and the "presentation" is a curated list AR objects that the presenter can switch between at will.

Main Presenter Experience

Problem To Be Solved

I worked on behalf of Fast Effect with one of their pre-existing clients, a car technology manufacturer in the Detroit, MI area. The client's problem was that it had purchased multiple Hololens units for use to demonstrate their products, primarily in a trade and tech show environment. In a forward-thinking effort, the client had purchased a number of Hololens units, and had previously asked Fast Effect to develop a directed tech show experience. This experience was ultimately judged to be a failure, and the Hololens units had been underutilized ever since.

My task was to determine the reasons for the failure, understand where the client wanted to next head with Augmented Reality, and design a solution for that new direction.

My Contribution


Stakeholder Analysis

I had a surprising (and enticing) level of freedom on this project, as the nature and scope of the project had not been defined when I joined Fast Effect. To provide this clarity, I first held stakeholder interviews with the client. I interviewed two primary stakeholders: the client's head of R&D marketing, and the head of community outreach. These interviews were held in a "contextual inquiry" manner, without a strict adherance to the interview protocol. This allows me to explore a wide range of topics related to the client's use and vision for augmented reality. With permission from the client, the interview's audio was recorded.

Once the interviews were complete, I introduced Fast Effects to the concepts of interpretation sessions and affinity walls. Previously at the company, the interview results would be informally reviewed by the company principles, and passed on to the technical team over time. However, for this project, I gathered the technical team to review the stakeholder interview directly. This proved to be a fruitful decision, as the combination of expertise allowed us to pull far more insight from the interview notes than we would have otherwise. We assembled these insights into a series of affinity notes.

Once the notes were completed, I lead the technical team in the creation of an affinity wall. From this affinity wall, we derived 4 design principles that guided the entire project from that point:

  1. A controlled, easy-to-use experience was paramount for the client AR app: The interviews revealed the previous AR app, while designed for trade/tech shows, had been repurposed as an outreach solution for student recruitment. This lead to the prospect that the new AR app would need to support a wide user base who possesed varying levels of AR expertise. In addition, both the tech show and outreach environments required high vistor throughput, which didn't leave time for even basic vistor training on app use. To navigate this client base, the application would need to be simple for the end-user to navigate, and highly-controllably by the client in order to guaruntee a controlled, positive experience.
  2. The client app should be a setup-free application that has minimal environment requirements:The primary point for this insight was that the client had virtually no in-house expertise in maintaining Hololens applications. Fast Effect had previously handled all aspects of deployment and maintenance during the initial AR app deployment, ,but the client had then used the app on its own, encountering multiple technical challenges, such poor lighting conditions, limited wireless access, and disrupted spatial tracking. Any solution we developed would need to ensure that the client could negotiate these challenges with minimal expertise.
  3. The client has a variety of use cases for AR/VR tech as a whole, with some use cases more developed than others:This insights relates to the various views within the client organization regarding AR technology. The marketings and outreach departments were already onboard with the use of AR technology as a method of promoting the client organization as a technology company first, and a manufacturing one second. However, many of the technical subject matter experts within the company, whose expertise would eventually be provide product specifications, had significant reservations about the use of AR. Fast Effect needed to be aware of these internal pressures as it built the application.
  4. The client app should be composed of parts that can be put together to tell an overall story:Throughout the entire stakeholder interview, the concept of a narrative was a central thread. This need manefisted itself in several ways. The client wanted to portray itself as forward-thinking, and wanted to use AR technology to "wow and dazzle" those who didn't know much about the client. The selection of the Hololens as a platform was built on this basis, as it boasted a futuristic-looking design, was not tethered, and was not isolating. However, the client did want the capability to create multiple narratives. During the interview, we provided the intial suggestion of "building" a presentation out of "parts", similar to how a car is constructed. The client took an immediate liking to this idea, even suggest a particular upcoming car technology system that could be used to prototype the modular approach.

Based on these insights, I developed four further recommendations to additional guide further design and development:

  1. Use only proven AR tracking technologies and avoid approaches that require the use of room-wide spacial mapping: The stakeholders revealed that we could not count on our client having access to a pre-defined, well-lit, stable venue environment to run a Hololens solution in. Based on this, we knew not to rely on room-wide spatial scans to trigger AR content. Instead, we knew to use Image and Model Targets as our primary triggers, or possibly some local, temporary location spatial anchors at certain specified locations.
  2. Avoid targeting specific show venue designs, and instead develop generic solutions that work in either desktop or wall-mounted modes: This was another recommendation based on the lack of defined, stable venues for the client app to run in. I recommended following up with the client to determine a minimum amount of desk and wall-mounted space to support an app environment, and then build packaged versions of the app to target those sizes.
  3. Further with the client to come up with a distinct target audience and a coherent message:As revealed by Insight #3, our client did not have an overall vision for its use of AR/VR tech, even within the public awareness sphere. One of the primary risks for the project is trying to satisfy so many potential stakeholders at the client organization that we wouldn't satisfy any of them to an acceptable degree. I recommended that we work further with the client to define a tight message and limited target user scope for the initial application.
  4. Build a modular storytelling approach into the upcoming app. While we could, and should limit the scope of the message and target audience for the application this summer, that does not mean we couldn't think ahead to some degree for future messaging and purpose. I recommended building a generic data structure backend that allows us to associate individual modules with a larger story. This data structure could then be adapted to serve other client needs at a rapid pace.

Ideation and Interaction Design

Persona Development

Now that we had our initial set of application criteria completed, it was time to start ideating to determine the form that the application should take. I began by developing personas based on our two known client users: trade show presenters and outreach presenters. I developed a persona for each user type. These personas were then used to ground all future brainstorming session. Paper copies were also kept near the technical team's working space to remind it of who our users were, and what their needs really were.

Brainstorming

After persona development, I once again gathered the technical team and facilitated a brainstormed session. The first phase of the session was completely free-form, with the intent to generate as many ideas as possible, without immediate worry of feasibility (a fact that I had to repeatedly remind the participants of, as the session progressed). Once we had hashed out an initial set of ideas, we discussed them more in-depth. We had several front-runner ideas emerging, including an AR scavenger hunt, a virtual visitation with a 3-D model of a client engineer, an "innovation timeline" (involving a virtual car that would change based on the client's innovations over the years), and the guided presentation experience that ended up becoming AR Presenter. Out of these ideas, the guided experience seemed to be the front-runner, as it aligned most directly with the insights and recommendations developed during the stakeholder analysis. Fast Effect's principles concurred with our selection of the guided experience.


Interaction Design

Thanks to the brainstorming, we had a solid initial idea, but we didn't have enough specifics to go to the client. I therefore worked out the overall interaction design for the application.
