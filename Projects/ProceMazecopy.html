VR "Clues" Maze

VR Attention Direction Within a Random Maze


View Process   View Final Output


Project Overview

Role: UX Designer, Full-Stack Developer  |  Duration: 4 Weeks
Tools Used: Unity Game Engine, SteamVR (for running the Maze on a VR headset)
UX Skills Used: VR Prototyping, Usability Testing, VR UX Design

Summary: Virtual Reality presents a unique challenge for immersive experiences, as the user has the freedom to look in all directions at any time. This freedom makes it possible for the user to be looking in the wrong place at the wrong time, and miss seeing key portions of the experience.

The VR Clues Maze is an immersive experience that balances user freedom and directed attention. The VR Clues Maze generates a unique layout every time the software is run. Dynamically added clues are then added to subtly direct the user out of the maze.

My Contributions: I co-designed the design of the VR Clues Maze with my partner on this project, Vlad Potiyevskiy. As co-designer, I was responsible for overall look-and-feel, which was a "Japanese-style" haunted house theme. I defined the overall look, developed the lighting setup, and sourced the assets required to achieve this theme.

I also acted as a developer on this project. In this role, I developed a pathfinding solution that could automatically find a path through the randomized maze. I then implemented a system to dynamically add clues around this path, providing the user guidance on how to get of the maze.


Process

How can a virtual environment subtly, but effectively direct the users attention?

My initial inspiration for this project was my experience with the Ghost in the Shell VR app. The app plays out like a short movie, centering around a chaotic fight scene in a large room. What I noticed is that since the experience was in a 3-D space, and I had full ability to look in all directions, I would often miss key parts of the fight scene, due to looking at the wrong direction at the wrong time. This brought on the realization that VR experiences need some ability to effectively direct the users attention, while still allowing the users complete viewing freedom.

The VR Clues Maze is a VR environment designed to direct the users attention, through lighting and subtle environment cues.

Here's the process we used to develop the Maze:

Scoping


Technical Exploration

Random Maze Generation

Vlad took on the task of exploring the Unity random maze tutorial, and bringing it into our VR environment. The procedural maze source was sound, and we were able to implement the majority of the maze in VR without major issues. The implementation initially treated the maze as a 2-D maze, generating a series of flat tiles. The implementation then adds walls, doors, and wall hangings to create a 3-D maze. My partner and I added additional wall and floor textures, as well as additional wall decorations, to make the maze more resemble the theming we were going for.

Dynamic Clue System

I began work on adding the dynamic lighting/clue system. First, I needed to design how this system would work. The system needed 3 main components:
  • A designed set of clues that could be used to hint at the correct direction to get out of the maze.
  • A system to determine the "path" the user should follow to get out of the maze.
  • A system to be able to dynamically insert clues around that path to give hints to users about how to get to the exit.


I first began by creating the initial design for what form the clues should look. I had several ideas for how the clues might look, including stone lamps, fireflies, and light-reflecting metallic shapes.

As the idea of lamp-based clues was the idea that I had come up with first, I decided to try that route first. I began by tuning the ambient lighting environment within the maze by adding a sourced night-sky skybox, and adjusting the overall ambient lighting to be a little dim. These adjustments allowed the user to see, but not incredibly well. This lighting situation would cause our eventual light-based clues and player-interactable torches to have immediate value to the player. I then sourced a Japanese lantern model, and created a maze module with this model, a light source, and a wall. We then added this module to the list of modules the random maze generator would use. Now, our maze would have lamps randomly-placed to provide a level of lighting support for the player.

Now that we had lamps integrated into the maze, I needed to find a way to make the lamps "clues" to guide the player out. It occurred to me as I built the initial lamps that a more subtle way to handle adding clues was to use design elements that were already present, but in new ways. I had sourced a Japanese Oni mask as part of a more generic wall module that we were using to establish a Japanese theme for the maze. I decided to combine the mask with the lantern prefab to create a clue. I created a new version of the lantern module identical to the original one, but with the exeception of having a tiny version of the Japanese Mask now embedded in it's side. The intent was to place these "clue" lanterns in addition to the random ones. While seeming random, these "clue" lanterns would actually be placed in the direction the user should go to get out of the maze.


Assessment

At the end of the technical exploration phase, Vlad and I assessed our status, and we rescoped the project. We had touched on most of the primary features we had isolated in our ideation phase, leaving only the Maze-Finding component of the Dynamic Clue System feature and the Player Interactions feature as unknowns. Our plan was to iterate to a full Version 1 of the Maze, with each one of us building out the rest of the two remaining features. Vlad took on the Player Interactions component, while I took on the Maze-Finding component.

Iteration

Iteration 1

Now that I had the visual design of a clue worked out, it was time to figure out how to place it. In order to intelligently In order to know where to place the clues, I would need to know where the optimal path was for each generated maze. This necessitated the need for a mazefinding algorithm I could run to find a path out, just after the maze was generated. I looked into several prexisting algorithms, such as Tremaux's and Pledge's, and tested an implementation of Pledge's in the Maze. However, these algorithms - they only worked for "simple" mazes that have only corridors. Our maze could generate small courtyard-style open spaces, and Pledge's failed when hitting one of those locations.

Going back to the drawing board, I decided to try using breadth-first search, as this algorithm was capable of handling the courtyard spaces. I was able to find several existing implementations of it for tile mazes, including one by the same individual that had developed the procedural maze code we were using. Based on these implementations, I was able to create an implementation of my own, scanning the tiles in the Maze, and marking the tiles that lead to the exit by changing their colors.

Now that I had a system with which to mark the path, I could start adding clues to mark the path, rather than the rather obvious color I was doing for debug purposes. The algorithm I developed for adding the path stepped through each tile on the path, and performed two actions:

  • The algorithm checked to see if the tile was a "third tile" - every third tile on the path was a candidate for placing a clue This would add just enough clues to be useful, without making the Maze too easy to get out of.
  • If the tile was in fact a "third" tile, the algorithm would check to see if the tile in question had a wall. If so, the algorithm replaced it with a wall that had a clue-lantern near it.
The algorithm was deployed just after the initial procedural generation, and would add the clue lanterns just before the user was able to exploring the maze.

Initial User Testing

As planned, we tested our Version 1 on users to gain initial feedback. We tested with 3 users, also participated in the design challenge. Testing consisted of undirected use of the Maze, with users playing the Maze on Rift headsets, giving feedback as they played. The user view was accessible via a separate monitor, which allowed for direct observation in addtion to feedback.

Our users had varying levels of VR background, but all had been trained in basic VR design best practices, which allowed them to give a strong mix of user/expert feedback.

User Feedback


Users used Lights As Navigation Aids

  • Users cited the lamps as the primary navigation aids they used to understand the maze structure.
  • This use confirmed the decision to use the lamps as the focus for the clue system.

Users Were Easily Disorientated

  • Users became easily disorientated when explored the maze.
  • Diorientation due to 4 design factors:
    • Floor textures were the same throughout the maze.
    • VR Teleporting moved users instantly through space.
    • The environment was too dark to pick out any landmarks.
    • The clue lanterns appeared to be showing up too infrequently to act as guides.

"Least Explored" Pathfinding Strategy

  • Users navigated the maze with a least-explored strategy, always attempting to go where they had not been before..
  • This strategy only had limited success, as users were not able to tell easily where they had been.

Users Wanted to Build a Mental Maze Map

  • Users stated a need to build an internal mental map of how the Maze was laid out, and were frustrated that this desire was not supported well by the app.
  • Users stressed the need to have "safe" landmarks to fall back on.

Iteration 2

For our final iteration, Vlad and I tried to address as many of the feedback points as possible, as well as adding two minor features for greater user enjoyment.


Iteration 2 Changes

Additional Clue Type

To address the need for more clues, we needed a clue type that could cover candidate spaces without walls. I created a module that contained a floor tile with a metal cube buried into it, and a faint light source positioned just above it to help the cube catch the light. I also increased the frequence of clues to every other tile.

End of User Celebration Area

I added a script to the end tile that would "teleport" the player to a separate area outside of the maze upon entry. I then added a Japanese-theme celebration area, with a sourced cherry-blossam tree, custom particle effects, and sources fire-fly themed Japanese lanterns.

Overhead View Implementation

We demoed the game using the Unity editor directly, and we noticed an interesting beheavior from our players (and ourselves). The Unity editor was set up to show an overhead view of the scene, which we had used for debugging purposes. However, demo-goers not wearing the headset were able to view the active player's position on the overhead view, and were able to guide the active player through the maze. We thought this was an interesting optional feature, and wanted to formally add it to the game.

Tutorial Area

My partner added an initial tutorial zone that the player enters prior to the maze itself. This tutorial area had instructions for playr controls, the goal of finding the end tiles, player interactables, and looking for clues.

Player Light

I added a dim light to the player module in the maze. This creates a small but constant light source that ensures the player can always see enough to navigate the maze.

Reflect


Post-Demo Analysis


Improved Maze Navigation

With the addition of the breadcrumbs, lighting, and additional clues, our users were able to navigate our maze far easier than Iteration 1. They were able to backtrack far easier, and reached the end of the maze with far more frequency.

The Overview Map allowed for a fun optional experience.

With the use of the overview map, we were able to add a second user as a helper to the experience. Users enjoyed the interplay between user and helper. The presence of the helper allowed users with weaker navigation skills to still enjoy the overall experience.

The Celebration Experienece Brought Proper Closure To The Experience.

With the celebration area, users had a clear feedback mechanism for telling that they had succeeded. The aesthestic design of the area also made it a fun reward for the users.


Clue Attention Direction

The clues remained of only limited use. While the greater frequency made them more likely to be noticed, the visual design was too subtle. Some users picked up on them, but some never recognized them at all.

Users Wanted More Interactions/Interesting Environments

Users who didn't find the maze exit relatively quickly tended to become bored with experience, once the novelty value had worn off. These users suggested having more interesting environment features (such as ponds and gardens) and activities to do (such as doors to unlocks, and people to talk to). We had ended up not included those features due to feasibility concerns, but their absence was noticed.



Potential Next Steps

My partner and I were pretty pleased on how the project turned out, but we are considering taking it further. We'd like to test the clue improvments made with further users, and continue working on the clue design and placement algorithm based on this feedback. We would also like to improve the look and feel of the maze, by modifying the pre-fabs to make the maze look more like an actual mansion. Finally, we'd like to add more player interaction, and give players other activities to do besides just navigating the maze.
