VR Clues Maze

VR Attention Direction Within a Random Maze


View Process   View Final Output


Project Overview

How can a Virtual Reality environment subtly, but effectively direct the users attention?

Role: UX Designer, Full-Stack Developer  |  Duration: 4 Weeks
Tools Used: Unity Game Engine, SteamVR (for running the Maze on a VR headset)
UX Skills Used: VR Prototyping, Usability Testing, VR UX Design

Summary: Virtual Reality presents a unique challenge for immersive experiences, as the user has the freedom to look in all directions at any time. This freedom makes it possible for the user to be looking in the wrong place at the wrong time, and miss seeing key portions of the experience.

The VR Clues Maze is an immersive experience that balances user freedom versus directed attention. The VR Clues Maze generates a unique layout every time the software is run. Clues are then intelligently added to subtly direct the user out of the maze.

My Contributions: I co-designed the VR Clues Maze with my partner on this project, Vlad Potiyevskiy. of the virtual environment, and developed the lighting setup required to achieve the desired atmosphere.

As developer, I developed a path-finding solution that could automatically find a path through the randomized maze. I then implemented a system to dynamically add clues around this path, providing the user guidance on how to get of the maze.


Process

My initial inspiration for this project was my experience with the Ghost in the Shell VR app. The app plays out like a short movie, centering around a chaotic fight scene in a large room. While using the app, I observed a repeated problem. Since I had full ability to look in all directions, I would often be looking in the wrong direction to see an important part of the scene. This brought on the realization that VR experiences need some ability to effectively direct the users attention, while still allowing the users complete viewing freedom.

The VR Clues Maze is a VR environment designed to direct the users attention through lighting and subtle environment cues.

Here's the process we used to develop the Maze:




Explore

Brainstorming

This project was occurring in the context of a design challenge with 4 weeks allowed for design and development. My partner and I had relatively free rein over the nature and content of the project, but we knew that scoping would be critical to meet our deadline. We therefore made a substantial effort during our first brainstorming session to prioritize our feature set.


Brainstormed VR Clues Maze Features

Oculus Rift/Unity Platform

  • The Oculus Rift headset was the most capable VR platform we had access to.
  • The Unity Game Engine for app development was the best balance of technical features and ease of use.

Randomly-Generated Maze

  • Our Maze would generate a random layout each time the app ran. This would add significant replayability to the app.
  • This feature would be a technical challenge, but we had a Unity tutorial to work off of.

Atmospheric Lighting

  • We saw the lights in the environment as a key attention direction tool.
  • We knew correct optimization of lighting was needed to ensure smooth VR performance.

Supernatural Theme

  • We went with a Japanese-based supernatural look-and feel, as this would provide a more unique immersive experience.
  • Supernatural theme would be kept "light" to avoid truly scaring users. We would avoid gore and shock value in our design.

Dynamic Clue System

  • To guide attention, we would build a system that would add clues to the maze. These clues would direct players on where to go.
  • Adding this feature to a randomly-generated maze was a significant design and technical challenge, as the solution would need to work without knowing the layout ahead of time.

Player Interactions

  • We did want player interactions, both as a design challenge, and to increase player immersion.
  • We knew that this area had the potential to overwhelm us .
  • Discussed interactions included:
    • Torches for extra lighting sources
    • Keys to open doors in the maze
    • Non-player characters (NPCs) for the player to interact with.

Scoping

How can we integrate a way to give player clues INTO a maze system that randomly changes layout every time the maze is created?

Technical Exploration


Our first major phase of work on the project was the Technical Exploration phase. In this phase, we would create proof-of-concepts versions of our two design pillars: Random Maze Generation, and a Dynamic Clue System. By creating these proof-of-concepts, we would be able to tell whether or not our project was feasible by our deadlines.

Random Maze Generation

Vlad took on the task of exploring the Unity random maze tutorial, and bringing it into our VR environment. The tutorial was sound, and we were able to implement the majority of the maze in VR without major issues. The implementation initially treated the maze as a 2-D maze, generating a series of flat tiles. The implementation then added walls, doors, and wall hangings to create a 3-D maze. My partner and I added additional wall and floor textures, to make the maze more resemble the theming we were going for.

Dynamic Clue System

While Vlad worked on the random generation system, I began work on adding the dynamic lighting/clue system. First, I needed to design how this system would work. The system needed 3 main components:
  • A designed set of clues that could be used to hint at the correct direction to get out of the maze.
  • A system to determine the "path" the user should follow to get out of the maze.
  • A system to dynamically insert clues around that path.


I first began by creating the initial design for what form the clues should look. I had several ideas for how the clues might look, including stone lamps, fireflies, and light-reflecting metallic shapes.

Out of the three options, I went with the stone lamp. The stone lamp balanced the use of a light to drive attention with the subtlety to look natural in our overall environment. I sourced an appropriate lamp model, and started integrating it into our VR environment.

I began by tuning the ambient lighting environment within the maze. I added a sourced night-sky skybox, and adjusting the overall ambient lighting to be a little dim. These adjustments allowed the user to see, but not incredibly well. This lighting situation would cause our eventual light-based clues and player-interactable torches to have immediate value to the player. I then created a maze module with the lamp, a light source, and a wall. We then added this module to the list of modules the random maze generator would use. Now, our maze would have lamps randomly-placed to provide a level of lighting support for the player.

Now that we had lamps integrated into the maze, I needed to find a way to make the lamps "clues" to guide the player out. It occurred to me as I built the initial lamps that a more subtle way to handle adding clues was to use design elements that were already present. I had sourced a Japanese Oni mask as part of a more generic wall module that we were using to establish a Japanese theme for the maze. I decided to combine the mask with the lantern prefab to create a clue. I created a new version of the lantern module, but with the exception of having a tiny version of the Japanese Mask now embedded in it's side. The intent was to place these "clue" lanterns in addition to the random ones. While seeming random, these "clue" lanterns would actually be placed in the direction the user should go to get out of the maze.


Scoping Assessment

At the end of the Technical Exploration phase, Vlad and I assessed our status. Our proof-of-concepts had worked out successfully, so we made the determination that our project could be completed by the deadline. Our next step was to iterate to a full Version 1 of the Maze, with each one of us building out proof-of-concepts of the two remaining features. Vlad took on the Player Interactions component, while I took on the Maze-Finding component of the Dynamic Clue System feature.


Iteration

Mazefinding Development


For the first development iteration, I needed to finish up the Dynamic Clue System. Now that I had the visual design of a clue worked out, it was time to figure out how to place the clue. To do this, I would need an algorithm to find a path out of each generated maze. I would then placed the clues along this path.

I looked into several pre-existing algorithms, such as Tremaux's and Pledge's, and tested an implementation of Pledge's in the Maze. However, these algorithms - they only worked for "simple" mazes that have only corridors. Our maze could generate small courtyard-style open spaces, and Pledge's failed when hitting one of those locations.

Going back to the drawing board, I decided to try using breadth-first search, as this algorithm was capable of handling the courtyard spaces. I was able to find several existing implementations of it for tile mazes, including one by the same individual that had developed the procedural maze code we were using. Based on these implementations, I was able to create an implementation of my own, scanning the tiles in the Maze, and marking the tiles that lead to the exit by changing their colors.

Now that I had a system with which to mark the path, I could start adding clues to mark the path. The algorithm I developed for adding the clues stepped through each floor tile on the path, and performed two actions for each tile:

  • The algorithm checked to see if the tile was a "third tile" - every third tile on the path was a candidate for placing a clue This would add just enough clues to be useful, without making the Maze too easy to get out of.
  • If the tile was in fact a "third" tile, the algorithm would . If so, the algorithm replaced it with a wall that had a clue-lantern.
The algorithm was deployed just after the initial maze generation, and would add the clue lanterns just before the user was able to exploring the maze.

Live Usability Testing

As planned, we tested our Version 1 on users to gain initial feedback. We tested with 3 users, also participating in the design challenge. Testing consisted of undirected use of the Maze, with users giving feedback as they played. The user's first-person view was accessible via a separate monitor, which allowed for direct observation.

Our users had varying levels of VR background, but all had been trained in basic VR design best practices, which allowed them to give a strong mix of user/expert feedback.

Live Usability Testing Feedback


Users used Lights As Navigation Aids

  • Users cited the lamps as the primary navigation aids they used to understand the maze structure.
  • This use confirmed the decision to use the lamps as the focus for the clue system.

Users Were Easily Disorientated

  • Users became easily disorientated when exploring the maze.
  • Disorientation was due to 5 design factors:
    • Floor textures were the same throughout the maze.
    • VR Teleporting moved users instantly through space.
    • The environment was too dark to pick out any landmarks.
    • The clue lanterns were used too infrequently to act as guides.
    • There was not enough feedback when the user reached the end of the maze.

"Least Explored" Path-finding Strategy

  • Users navigated the maze with a least-explored strategy, always attempting to go where they had not been before.
  • This strategy only had limited success, as users were not able to tell easily where they had been.

Users Wanted to Build a Mental Maze Map

  • Users were frustrated that the app did not support their need to build an internal mental map of the Maze.
  • Users stressed the need to have "safe" landmarks to fall back on, so that they were never totally lost.

Feedback Iteration

For our final iteration, Vlad and I tried to address as many of the feedback points as possible, as well as adding two minor features for greater user enjoyment.


Feedback Iteration Changes

Additional Clue Type

To address the need for more clues, I added a new clue type that could be embedded into floor tiles. I also increased the frequency of clues to every other tile.

End of User Celebration Area

To provide better end-of-maze feedback, I added a Japanese-themed celebration area that the user would see upon maze completion.

Overhead View Implementation

We demoed the game using the Unity editor directly, and we noticed an interesting behavior from users. Demo-goers not wearing the headset were able to view the active player's position on the overhead view in Unity, and could guide the active player through the maze. We added this as a formal feature to optionally aid navigation.

Tutorial Area

My partner added an initial tutorial zone that the player enters prior to the maze itself. This tutorial area had instructions for player controls, the goal of finding the end of the maze, player interactables, and the need to look for clues.

Player Light

To address the darkness of the environment, I added a dim light to the player module. This created a small but constant light source that guaranteed the player could always see.

Final User Walkthrough


We conducted a second round of live usability testing on Version 2 of the Maze. 10 participants of similar background to the first testing participated. Several of the primary users were paired with a second participant who used the Overhead View to guide the user around the maze.

Reflect


Post-Demo Analysis


Improved Maze Navigation

With the addition of the tutorial, lighting, and additional clues, our users were able to navigate our maze far easier than Version 1. They were able to backtrack far easier, and reached the end of the maze with far more frequency.

The Overview Map allowed for a fun optional experience.

With the use of the overview map, we were able to add a second user as a helper to the experience. Users enjoyed the interplay between user and helper. The presence of the helper allowed users with weaker navigation skills to still enjoy the overall experience.

The Celebration Experience Brought Proper Closure To The Experience.

With the celebration area, users had a clear feedback mechanism for telling that they had succeeded. The aesthetic design of the area also made it a fun reward for the users.


Clue Attention Direction

The clues remained of only limited use. While the greater frequency made them more likely to be noticed, the visual design of the clues was too subtle. Some users picked up on them, but some never recognized them at all.

Users Wanted More Interactions/Interesting Environments

Users who didn't find the maze exit relatively quickly tended to become bored with experience. These users suggested having more interesting environment features and activities to do. We had ended up not included those features due to feasibility concerns, but their absence was noticed.
