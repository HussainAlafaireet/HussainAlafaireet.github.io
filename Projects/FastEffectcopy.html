AR Presenter

AR presentation for the chaotic trade show environment


View Process   View Final Output


Project Overview

Role: Product Designer  |  Duration: 8 Weeks
Tools Used: Unity (initial prototyping, networking), Hololens Toolkit (connecting Hololens to Unity), Microsoft Sharing Services (used to establish app spatial tracking)
UX Skills Used: User/Stakeholder Interviews, Personas, Storyboarding, Prototyping , Stakeholder Pitching

Summary: AR Presenter, developed by Fast Effect in Summer 2018, is a Hololens-based Augmented Reality software used to demonstrate products in trade/tech show venues. AR Presenter uses a careful combination of local-area location sharing, simplified presenter control, and modular AR presentation construction to defeat the the problems typical AR products face when used in the show environment.

My Contributions: As the AR Product Design Lead, I was the guiding light behind the AR Presenter prototype. I conducted stakeholder research to determine the real user needs, generated the interaction design for AR Presenter, and oversaw it's development cycle. Along the way, I introduced Fast Effect to the Interpretation Session and Affinity Wall user-centered design technique, as well as fostering an enhanced degree of ownership and teamwork within an interdisciplinary development team.


Process

The typical AR app performs badly in the trade show environment.

I worked on behalf of Fast Effect with one of their pre-existing clients, a car technology manufacturer in the Detroit, MI area. The client had multiple Hololens wireless AR headsets, and had previously tried to perform product demonstrations with them via an AR app. The client had not been satisfied with the overall expereince of this previous app, and the Hololens had been underutilized ever since.

My task was to determine the reasons for the disatisfaction, understand where the client wanted to next head with Augmented Reality, and design a solution for that new direction.

Here's the process I used to arrive at AR Presenter:


Explore

Stakeholder Interviews

The client needed an AR app that was visitor-friendly, easy to maintain, and venue-agnostic.
In order to understand the previous app history and it's corresponding effect on the stakeholder desires for a new app, I conducted a set of semi-structured stakeholder interviews. I interviewed the heads of the two client departments using the previous app: The head of R&D marketing, and the head of community outreach. With permission from the client, the interview's audio was recorded.

Previous App Review

The Fast Effect development team had also observed the previous client team in action, and their overall observations overlapped significantly with the client observations. In addition, the development team was able to provide technical explanations as to why the issues observed had actually occured. This would be of great help when I began the design process for AR Presenter, as I was able to avoid these technical challenges from the start.



Analysis


Affinity Wall

Once the exploration activities were complete, I introduced Fast Effect to the concepts of interpretation sessions and affinity walls. During the interpretation session, I led a facilitated review of the audio recordings pulling on my UX experience and the team's institutional knowledge of the client to identify important points. We then conducted an affinity wall session to organize and categorize these key points, deriving 4 key findings.


Interview Analysis Findings


The app experience had to work with minimal AR expertise

  • The client's end users had minimal AR app experience.
  • High visitor throughput left no time to train even basic app use.

The client app should adapt to venue space with minimal setup effort

  • Trade show environment presents technical issues for Hololens apps
    • Poor lighting
    • Limited Wireless Access
    • Traffic flow disrupting spatial tracking
  • Client reps didn't have the expertise to troubleshoot such challenges



Long-term app success required consideration of multiple use cases

  • Client org transitioning from manufacturing to technology firm.
  • AR used for both technical product demonstration and student outreach.
  • Client technical experts skeptical about the use of AR.

The client app must be capable of telling compelling stories

  • Client wanted to portray itself as forward-thinking and wanted to use
  • Client wanted AR technology to "wow and dazzle" visitors
  • Client was positive to the notion of "building" a presentation out of "car parts"
Based on these insights, I developed four design pillars to additionally guide further design and development.

Design Pillars

Use only proven AR tracking technologies, and avoid room-wide spacial mapping:

Avoid as many assumptions about venue format as possible, and build adaptability into the design



Tailor the initial prototype to a specific client message

Build a modular storytelling approach into the app.


Personas

Now that we had our initial set of application criteria completed, it was time to start ideating to determine the form that the application should take. I began by developing personas based on our two known client users: trade show presenters and outreach presenters. I developed a persona for each user type. These personas were then used to ground all future brainstorming session. Paper copies were also kept near the technical team's working space to remind it of who our users were, and what their needs really were.

Ideation

User-Centered Brainstorming + Compelling Team Ownership = Design Success.

Team Brainstorming

After persona development, I once again gathered the technical team and facilitated a brainstormed session. The first phase of the session was completely free-form, with the intent to generate as many ideas as possible, without immediate worry of feasibility (a fact that I had to repeatedly remind the participants of, as the session progressed). Once we had hashed out an initial set of ideas, we discussed them more in-depth. We had several front-runner ideas emerging, including an AR scavenger hunt, a virtual visitation with a 3-D model of a client engineer, an "innovation timeline" (involving a virtual car that would change based on the client's innovations over the years), and the guided presentation experience that ended up becoming AR Presenter. Out of these ideas, the guided experience seemed to be the front-runner, as it aligned most directly with the insights and recommendations developed during the stakeholder analysis. Fast Effect's principles concurred with our selection of the guided experience.


Design

The final design was a modular presentation app, with powerful, adaptive storytelling front and center.

Interaction Design

Thanks to the brainstorming, we had a solid initial idea, but we didn't have enough specifics to go to the client. I therefore worked out the overall interaction design for the application. I built an initial set of interaction design sketches that outline the main interaction areas of the application:



Proposed AR Presenter Features


Adaptive Location Selection

In order to navigate a potentially confusing trade/tech show environment, the presenter has the option of placing a "marker" hologram to designate the physical location that best suits the presentation at hand.

Controlled Presenter Experience

While in presentation mode, the presenter has full control over the experience, with the ability to switch between pieces of AR content, and the ability to trigger specific interactions on them, which are then reflected in the presentee view in real-time.

Voice Chat

Trade and Tech Show environments can be quite loud. AR Presenter implements voice chat through the Hololens microphone and speaker system to compensate for the noise.

Standalone Mode

In situations where a presenter isn't available, presentations can be run in a "standalone" mode, with presentees able to use simplified versions of the presenter controls to walk through the experience.

"Parts" Ecosystem

The presentation system is modular. The AR Presenteer app has a dedicated mode for building presentations, utilizing a library of AR content that can be expanded as needed.

"Safe" Shared Experience Environment

AR Presenter utilizes a sharing experience revolving around a local wireless router packaged with the system, married to a single shared anchor placed when the presentation is started. This ensures a usable shared experience even in crowded venue spaces with poor lighting conditions.


Lo-Fi Prototyping

To clarify the vision of AR Presenter to our client, I developed a series of lo-fi Photoshop shots for use in a client pitch presentation. These shots, while still preliminary, created a clear, compelling view of AR Presenter and it's primary features.

Client Pitch Presentation

After the initial interaction design had been worked out, it was time to present the AR Presenter concept to the client. I prepared a pitch presentation deck, which included the main insights we had learned from the Stakeholder Analysis, mockups of the primary views in the application, and both a short-term plan (to develop an initial prototype), and a long-term plan (to develop secondary features an an ecosystem of AR content).

The client was overall pleased with the AR Presenter concept, and authorized the creation of an initial prototype. The client also provided additional Feedback on the concept, which was incorporated into the planning for the initial prototype.


Develop

We were able to develop a successful prototype in a minimal amount of time, thanks to the correct design and strong team cohesion.

Sprint Planning

My next task was to plan the development cycle for the intial AR prototype. We had committed to having the prototype in two weeks, which meant that implementing all of the proposed features of AR Presenter was not feasible. I isolated a subset of features that, when implemented, would show off the capabilities of the design. These features were:

  • Adaptive Location Selection: This feature would shoe that our prototype could handle the trade show environment.
  • Controlled Presenter Experience: This feature is the core of the AR Presenter user experience, and was required to give the client a realistic idea of what the final app would be like.
  • Voice Chat: Previous explorations of Hololens tech had shown that this feature would be relatively simple to implement.
I developed plans for two one-week sprints. These sprints were broken down by functional area, with Graphic Design, Application Development, and 3-D Artist each receiving tasks. My role within the sprints was two-fold. I acted as overall project/design lead, with final say over any design decisions that needed to be made during the sprints. I also acted as a secondary application developer, assisting the primary developer on the creation of the core application functionality.

I also worked with the rest of the team to design our specific use case scenario for the demo. The demo would revolve around a particular new auto technology system that our client had recently developed. The scenario would include two main AR objects: a detailed, close-up car to introduce the system, and a wide-shot road simulation to show how the technology would work in action.

Once I had developed a plan, I held a kick-off meeting with the technical team to present the client feedback and sprint plan. Having this formal meeting ensured that all members of the technical team were on the same page, and gave them a venue to check the feasibility of the sprint plan and ask for any additional resources they needed.

User Journey Design

Providing this venue was a good move, as the primary developer requested from me a further breakdown of the application design, with more granualar steps than the initial set of design sketches. I provided this breakdown as my initial task during the sprint.

App Development

During the AR prototype development sprints, I contributed to the development code in the following areas:

  1. I developed the high-level logic for the custom network messages functionality that would allow the presenter Hololens to send messages to the presentee Hololens. These messages were required to ensure that the presentees would see presenter changes in real-time.
  2. I implemented voice chat functionality, based on code examples from the Microsoft Hololens team. This functionality would allow presenters to deal with loud trade show environments by being able to send their voice directly to the presentee Hololens.
  3. I assisted the primary programmer on the implementation of the Sharing service. This service, again based on Microsoft code, would allow the presenter to place and run their presentation at a specified location in the physical environment. The primary challenge here was actually stripping functionality out of the Sharing service. The service normally scans a physical environment repeatedly in order to maintain and update its understanding, but our stakeholder analysis had revealed that this repeated scanning caused problems in a highly-trafficked environment. We wanted the Sharing service to scan the room only once (at the beginning of the session) and to forgot all physical environment data between presentations.

Final Output

Demo


Client Walkthrough

At the end of the second week of sprints, we invited our two client stakeholders in to test our demo. We walked the client through our demo scenario and then allowed them to experiment with the menu system, voice chat, and shared AR object control. The client was pleased with the demo's capabilities, and authorized the creation of a full version of AR Presenter.

Reflect

The proof-of-concept demo sucessfully achieved the primary objective: securing funds for the full version of AR Presenter.

Post-Demo Analysis

However, even with this success, I still needed to assess the lessons learned from both the development and demonstration processes. My analysis yielded the following insights:

Shared Presenter/Presentee Functionality

The client was particularly impressed with the demo's ability to have the presenter's actions immediately reflect on the presentee hologram. They quite clearly saw the power of AR Presenter as a controlled live experience.

Menu Design Offset and Sizing

We had carefully sized the presentation controls to be easily usable via Hololens gestures, and placed the controls so that the user could easily turn right to see them, without having them obscure the presentation content. Client feedback on this arrangement was positive, confirming the correctness of the menu design.

"Wow and Dazzle" Visual Fidelity

The client was impressed by the immersive factor of our demo scenario, and was onboard with using AR Presenter to tell compelling narratives.


Attention Direction

The client had difficulty knowing precisely where in the road scenario they should be looking at a particular time. Future scenarios would need to be much more clear as to where the "action" was happening.

Left-Handed Presenter Controls

The client pointed out that left-handed users might be less comfortable with the right-positioned presentation controls. We would need to offer a left-aligned menu as an option.

"Scalable Holograms"

The client stated a desire to have the presentation holograms be scalable, so that they could be adapted to the presentation area in use.


Project Plan

The final phase of my assignment was designing a project plan to govern the development of the full version of AR Presenter. I suggested the full version be broken down into 3 main phases:
  1. Phase 1: Demo clean-up and full presentation scenario: In this phase, Fast Effect would implement the Standalone Mode, the Scaleable Holograms, and the Left-Handed Presentation Controls features. It would also work with the client to develop a full product demonstration scenario that the client could start using, even as the rest of the app was being developed.
  2. Phase 2: Parts Ecosystem Proof of Concept: In this phase, Fast Effect would develop all of the backend code required to support the Part Ecosystem, as well as developing a set of AR objects to demostrate what this system could really do.
  3. Phase 3: "Safe Shared Environment" Finalization and Final UI AdjustmentsIn this phase, Fast Effect would finalize the local network functionality required for the Safe Shared Experience Environment feature, and would test this feature in an actual show venue. Fast Effect would also use this phase to finalize any features that needed polish.
With this final deliverable, I had successfully completed my assignment. Fast Effect and their client were well on their way to developing a cutting-edge AR experience together.
