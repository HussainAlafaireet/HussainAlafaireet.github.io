Literature Reflection: "Usability engineering of virtual environments (VEs): identifying multiple criteria that drive effective VE system design"

Stanney, Mollaghasemi, Reeves, Breaux, & Graeber, 2003

The Main Ideas

This article covers the characteristisc and backend methodology behind the Multi-criteria Assessment of Usability for Virtual Environments (MAUVE), a technique for systematically assessing the usability of VR environments. MAUVE is intended to cover a set of perceived gaps in applying traditional usability techniques in VR:

  1. VR interactions often don't resemble the traditional "point and click" interactions of typical software.
  2. VR applications typically use multiple modes of output simultaneously, and traditional usabiliy techniques don't consider this fusion of output.
  3. VR applications require a strong sense of immersion and presence to be effective. Traditional usability methods don't assess these characteristics directly.
  4. VR system interaction performance can't be adequately measured by the time/accuracy metrics that are the gold standard for traditional UX.
  5. Traditional usability assessment lacks coverage on multiple users in a shared VR environment.

The MAUVE Model

The MAUVE technique is built around a hierarchical model of the usability characteristics associated with VR environments. This model, in this UX professional's assessment, is the most valuable component of the article, as the model provides a comprehensive way of thinking about VR usability. The individual model components break down as follows:

VE System Interface

The VE System Interfaces characteristic refer to the software and hardware characteristics present in any VR environment design.

Interaction

This characteristic covers the interactions users can engage a VR environment with. Overall, the idea here is that users can engage in basically two types of activities: they can move with the virtual environment, or they can interact with an object in the environment. MAUVE more specifically breaks down Interaction into the following sub-areas:

  • Wayfinding: Wayfinding refers to the users ability to create a mental map of the virtual environment, tell where they are within the environment, and how well the user can make decisions about where to go next. Usabiliy considerations for wayfinding include the correct use of visual cues in the environment (such as the clear usages of paths and landmarks), as well as the availability of navigation aids (such as a map or virtual signage).
  • Navigation: Navigation refers to the users ability to actually move in the virtual environment, once they decide where to go. Usability considerations in this area are only lightly discussed in the article, but include such considerations as accuracy of movement, how natural the movement feels, and how streamlined the interaction is.
  • Object Selection and Manipulation: This characteristic refers to the users abilty to select and manipulate objects within the VR environments. Usability considersations relative to this this characteristic include the degree of realism used, the modifications made to virtual objects to make manipulation easier (such as altering the color of an object when selecting), and the accuracy of the virtual environments user gesture tracking. Stanney et al. also note that the utter lack of standardization in conventions for how object selection/manipulation is implemented contributes to usability issues.

Multi-Modal System Output

This characteristic covers the various output methods VR environments use to convey information and feedback to users. This characteristic can be broken down into the following sub-characteristics:

  • Visual Output: This characteristic refers to the visual presentation of information within the virtual environment. Usability considerations within this area include primarily the technical decision made to support visual output, such as the screen resolution, the width of the users field of view, and the refresh rate of the visual display.
  • Auditory output: This refers to the audio presentation of information in the virtual environment. Usability considerations in this area include the quality of the spatial positioning of audio content, the effect the users surrounding environmet has on the audio content, and the use of audio in conjunction with visual output.
  • Haptic Output: This characteristic refers to the use of tactile or force feedback. The article does not go into deep detail into the specific usability concerns in this area.

VE User Interface

The VE User Interface characteristic refers to the human physical and mental considerations that should be accounted for in VR design. Note that the terminology used for this characteristic can be a little misleading when considered in the context of general usability discussion. "User Interface" in traditional usability discussion often refers to the actual Graphical User Interface (GUI), which, in the MAUVE model, falls under the "Interaction" characteristic of VE System Interface.

Engagement

This characteristic refers to the VR environments ability to encourage and motivate the user to focus on the virtual environment, and suspend their sense of disbelief. This characteristic can be broken down into the following sub-characteristics:

  • Presence: While a notoriously hard term to define precisely, this article defines presence as "the subjective perception of experiencing oneself as being in a computergenerated environment rather than in one’s actual physical location" (Stanney et al. 2003). The article notes that while many in the VR space assume that presence is needed to make a VR environment effective, there was little systematic evidence at the time the article was written to support this. Under the premise that presence is in fact needed, usability considerations such as the realism of environment details, the ease of interaction with the environment, and the amount and nature of user-initiated control the environment provides.
  • Immersion: Immersion refers to the ability of the VR environment to isolate the user from the actual physicial environment they are in, creating the impression that the virtual environment is the only environment that currently exists. Usability considerations in this area include how well the physical hardware isolates the user, how natural the interactions feel, ad how available environmental visual/audio output is to create the impression of a true environment.

Side Effects

This characteristic covers the various side effects that VR environments can impart on their users. This characteristics can be broken down into the following sub-characteristics:

  • Comfort: Comfort, in this context, refers to the general presence of physical comfort and safety can provide. Usability concerns within this area include avoiding using incorrectly designed depth cues, as well as color contrast and illumination issues.
  • Sickness: This characteristics refers primarily to the VR-triggerd motion sickness that affects a substantial portion of the potential user population. MAUVE break the Sickness category down into a variety of discrete symptoms that can be used as assessment criteria.
