Project Overview

How do we get University students to actually exercise regularly?

Role: UX Designer, UX Researcher, AR Developer  |  Duration: 4 Months
Tools Used: Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Premiere, AFrame Virtual/Augmented Reality web framework, AR.JS Augmented Reality library, Youtube Gaming video streaming solution
UX Skills Used: UX Design, UI Design, UX Research, Interviewing, AR/VR Prototyping, AR/VR Code Development, Client Pitching

Summary: Life as a Gym (LG) is a set of Internet-connected AR glasses that help busy University students maintain exercise routines. LG scans the users environment during their daily routines, and identifies opportunities for on-the-spot exercise. LG coordinates its suggestions with the users schedule, seemlessly inserting exercise into the users lives.

My Contributions: I worked with three other UX Designers to develop the design prototype for LG. I was the team lead for the project, leading the design vision, and establishing the timetable for all activities. I was also a primary UX Designer/Researcher for the project. As designer, I crafted the core concept of LG. As researcher, I helped develop all user research for the project. Finally, I was the developer of the LG prototype, utilizing AR prototyping tools to create a compelling demonstration of our design.


Process

I worked with three other UX professionals on a design challenge that resulted in Life as a Gym (LG): AR smart glasses to support exercise in the life of a busy University student. LG works by scanning the environment in the users vicinity, and finding opportunities for informal exercise routines. By marrying these exercise scans with an understanding of the users' schedule, LG makes finding the time and place for exercise effortless.

We had 4 months to design and create our prototype, with a demonstration session in front of stakeholders as the final target.

Here's the process we used to design LG:




Ideation

Defining Problem Space

This design challenge allowed us the freedom to develop a smart device design for any domain we wished. Our first step was to define the problem we wanted to address with our design. We began by brainstorming problems together, and then recognizing common threads in the brainstorming. Our common threads were built based on our collective previous experiences as University students. Our common desire was to create a device that would address one of the many health concerns that University students face.

Brainstorming

Once we had defined University Student Health as our overall problem, we began developing initial design concepts to further refine the problem space. As we developed our concepts, we noticed that, in addition to the University health scoping, we were also coming up with functionality that revolved around memory and the use of reminders to manage one's activity.

Based on the two brainstorming pillars of University Student Health and Reminders we developed several more fleshed-out concepts. My contribution to the concept pool was a smart gym concept:

Once again, we began to notice trends in our brainstorming. We found that exercise was a common concern in our design. We therefore refined our problems space to Helping University Students manage their exercise time. We anticipated, based on our own experience, that University students faced many challenges in working exercise into their daily routines.

Research

Now that we had an refined problem space developed, we began developing a user research plan to better understand the challenges associated with exercise time management. We first developed our core research questions for the study.

Research Questions

What motivates the target audience to exercise?

This question was targeted at learning what factors got our users to actually exercise. Our design could then work at enhancing these motivations.

What factors prevent target audience from exercising?

This question was targeted at learning what barriers prevented our users from exercising. Our design could then work at breaking down these barriers.

How does target audience learn how to exercise?

This question was targeted at understanding whether our audience was learning to do exercises correctly and efficiently. Our design could be targeted at learning how to perform exercise more easily, leading to a greater likelihood of actually exercising.

What kinds of exercises does our target audience actually do?

By understanding what exercises our target audience tended to use, we could narrow the focus of our design to support those exercises specifically.

What resources does our target audience use to enable their exercise?

With this question, we wanted to understand what tools users were already using to enable their exercise routines, and what flaws those resources had. Our design could then address those flaws.

Study Design

To determine the answers to our research questions, we designed two complimentary studies:



Research Analysis

We had a rough mapping of survey and diary questions relative to their corresponding research question. To analysis our results, we first tabulated our quantitative data and performed light affinity wall analysis to categorize our qualitative data from the diary. We then formalized this mapping during our post-survey analysis, directly relating the results of each study question back to a core research question.

By having this mapping viewable on a whiteboard as a whole, we were also able to see patterns between the different activities and questions, which lead us to three core insights:



Lack of time/time management

  • Most prevalent and important finding.
  • Time was both primary exercise concern and primary exercise metric
    • Participants measured amount of exercise in time rather than effort.
    • Time cited as primary barrier to exercise completion.

Endurance and Strength exercises were the most prominent forms of exercise.

  • Running and biking were most common exercises
  • Weight training and floor exercises next most common.

Users often need guidance when doing exercises to learn how to do them correctly

  • Significant amount of discussion in both survey and diary study about exercise education needs.
  • Particularly apparent in the use of strength exercise, where proper form is particularly important, both for effectiveness, and to prevent injury.

Design

Revised Ideation

Based on our new primary design insights, we generated 3 new design concepts:

Smart Exercise Mat

  • Exercise Mat with built-in instructional screens and lights
  • Helps people learn proper form
  • Portable nature adds convenience

Smart Gesture Guide

  • Portable camera/sensor unit that observes users as they exercise, and provides guidance.
  • Built-in screen and speaker allows for multiple instruction methods

Life As a Gym

  • AR Glasses with ability to scan environment for on-the-spot exercise
  • Connected to users' calender to ensure on-the-spot exercises fit within daily schedule

Final Design Selection

We selected the Life as a Gym concept for two main reasons. Firstly, we felt that out of our three solutions, the glasses best addressed the primary consideration of time management. Secondly, we were the most interested in the concept from a creative standpoint, due to the futuristic nature of using AR technology to enable physical exercise.

User Enactments

Thanks to our initial user studies, we had a solid understanding of the primary factors affecting how our target audience approaches physical exercise, and "Life as a Gym" (LG) - a design concept that directly addressed those factors. However, we knew we needed user input to understand the exact set of features LG should have.

To determine these features, we we decided to conduct User Enactments - sessions where users experience the concept by acting out likely scenarios for LG use. User Enactments were extremely helpful for our specific concept because LG revolved around a new technology that many people have yet to interact with.

User Enactment Analysis

We then converted our primary findings into a list of 8 design principles we would use to develop our prototype:

LG Design Principles

Focus on individual usage scenarios

According to our research, users feel uncomfortable with involving their friends in LG activities. Most of them would not use this feature in social settings. Therefore, we decided to focus on individual usage scenarios

Recommending walking and running as primary exercises, climbing as secondary

We found that most users were comfortable with walking, running, or climbing as public activities. Therefore, we decided to focus on these three opportunity areas.

Display information visually and verbally, and support user input with voice and gesture control

Users worked with voice, text and gesture controls extensively in our study. However, the amount of text was too much for users to read and often impeded the users sight. We would therefore reduce the text in our app to a minimum amount, and place text to the side of the users central view.

Provide multiple choices

Users preferred having moderate levels of choice, particularly in the realms of route selection and user profile preference.

Only track exercise-relevant data

Users had privacy concerns and did not want LG to access their friend lists, contact information and any other personal background information. Our design would only track user calories and exercises for user reference.

Provide calories, time, and health relevant information

Based on user feedback, calories burned was the most important health information to be tracked. Users also wanted to track how much time a given exercise route would take, as well as how much time they had in their schedule.

Provide enough guidance for onboarding and data usage

LG represents powerful, emerging technology that our users had only a limited experience base to understand with. Our onboarding needed to introduce base AR concepts, as well as clarify data use.

Non-Distracting User Interface

UI design should be optimized to support the visual experience of walking/running. UI design should be non-intrusive and minimalistic, allowing the user to maintain situational awareness of the real-world environment.

System Proposal

Our final system proposal was a time-management system designed to integrate cardiovascular exercise into the daily non-exercise routine of a university student. As the student goes about their daily exercise activity, the system will scan their calendar system and compare it with location data to generate ad-hoc walking/running routines that still fit with the students schedule.

This system consists of the following key features:

Key LG Features


AR Cameras

Actively scan the environment and allows the AR interaction to occur.

Time & Schedule Integration

LG integrates with your electronic calendar and knows when you need to be somewhere.

Navigation

LG acts as a personal navigation device with turn-by-turn guidance through any activities you perform.

Voice Assistance & Input

Users can hear LG glasses speak, and they can interact with the glasses using voice.

Exercise Moments

LG smartly manages your time, and recommends exercises that fit into your schedule.

Companion App

LG comes with a companion application that gives users more in-depth information about their daily exercise.

Prototype

Now that we had our overall design concept in place, complete with user input on specific feature areas, we began building a prototype to demonstrate LG.

Prototype Construction

As the resident AR expert on the team, I was the one primarily responsible for the design and implementation of the initial prototype. As we had 4 weeks to develop a demonstration prototype, my plan was to create a "Wizard of Oz" prototype that would simulate the actual intended experience. To do this, I built an AR app using AFrame (a web-based AR/VR framework), and AR.JS (a free AR utility library). This app would simulate the LG experience using an ordinary smartphone. The app ran on a webpage that the phone would access via a standard browser. The app accessed the phone's camera to create a live video feed. The webpage could also layer several configurations of user interface elements on top of the video feed. Each UI configuration represented a particular feature of LG. These configurations could be switched by a tap on the phone screen.

This setup allowed us to create a convincing demonstration of LG via a video livestream from the phone. By holding the phone at head-height, switching between the UI configurations, and livestreaming the phone output, we created a demonstration of the LG design concept with convincing fidelity.

Reflect

Post-Demo Analysis

First Person Video Mockups are a powerful way to convey AR Design

We found that both the promotional video and the Wizard-of-Oz livestream communicated our design effectively, due to the use of first-person view. In addition, we found that both mockup methods could generate a demo prototype in short amounts of time.

Our User Enactments allowed us to discover edge cases

Our user enactments were easily the most effective research methods used in our project. The in-depth discussion they sparked allowed us to isolate features and issues we would never have thought of ourselves.

Intermingling design and research sessions informed both effectively

We found that the early use of initial designs allowed us to inform our initial research design, which then fed back into our next design steps. Repeating this process over and over ensured that all of our activities had a basis that would lead to success.

Sketch-based AR prototyping remains a challenge

Our attempts to use sketches as the basis of our user enactments had only limited success in conveying the experience of LG. We had to verbally explain concepts of our design far more than expected. In addition, we found that holding sketches during the enactment caused users to focus on the test facilitators, which may have biased our users towards using voice input.

Safety issues for LG use remain a concern

Enactment users discussed safety issues regarding attention direction in busy environments, privacy concerns, and late-night street usage. While we took these issues into account when building the final LG design, our modifications remain untested in terms of actually providing safety.

A physical AR glasses prototype proved infeasible

Our original intent was to have a Wizard-of-Oz prototype based on a physical set of glasses that Final Demo attendees could try themselves. This plan proved to be infeasible due to unforseen technical challenges. While our livestream mock-up worked well, we still felt that a physical prototype would have conveyed our vision to a greater degree.
